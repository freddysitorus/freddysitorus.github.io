Research

The Economic Impact of Village Funds and Pre-Existing BUMDes: A Difference-in-Differences Analysis with Evidence from East Java

This paper examines how institutional capacity influences the effectiveness of fiscal decentralization using Indonesia’s Village Fund policy as a case study. Employing a difference-in-differences approach, it finds that villages with pre-existing BUMDes before the 2015 policy experienced a 53% greater increase in log village income compared to those without, even after controlling for key characteristics. The results are supported by event study and parallel trend analyses, and further show that the benefits are strongest in medium-poverty villages and those led by more educated leaders, highlighting the importance of absorptive capacity and leadership quality. Conversely, impacts are weaker in high-poverty and densely populated villages, indicating the need for more tailored interventions, such as increased funding for larger populations. Overall, the findings suggest that successful decentralization depends on institutional readiness, offering broader lessons for countries where weak local capacity has limited policy effectiveness.

Mobile learning, Lasting Impact: from Digital Upskilling to Economic Empowerment for Rural Women in Kenya: A Case Study of One Acre Fund

This study examines One Acre Fund’s (OAF) digital transformation, in partnership with the Gates Foundation, to digitize its female field force and support rural women farmers in developing microenterprises through the Learn.Ink platform. Using qualitative data from fieldwork conducted in March 2025 across three regions in Kenya (Webuye, Nambilima, and Koteku), including one focus group and 16 semi-structured interviews, the research evaluates two workstreams: digital engagement of field officers and women’s microenterprise development. While initial adoption of digital tools was high, sustained engagement was constrained by time poverty, connectivity challenges, data costs, limited device access, and low digital confidence. Findings indicate that digital interventions can enhance women’s economic empowerment only when aligned with users’ lived realities and supported by enabling conditions. The study highlights the importance of accessible training design and supportive delivery mechanisms, and offers insights for scaling gender-sensitive digital programs in similar contexts.
